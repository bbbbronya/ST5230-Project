ST5230 Project — Conclusion
============================

Research Question:
  How sensitive are LLM uncertainty estimates to semantically equivalent
  prompt perturbations?

Models:  Ministral-3B (open-source, 3B)
         Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (open-source, 8B)
         GPT-4o (proprietary)

Datasets: SciQ (250 samples), TruthfulQA (250 samples)
Prompt Variants: V0 (baseline), V1 (reworded), V2 (question-first), V3 (minimal)

===============================================================================
PART I — DETAILED CONCLUSION
===============================================================================

1. Overall Stability
--------------------

Prediction Flip Rate (the proportion of samples whose predicted answer changes
across the 4 prompt variants) reveals a clear hierarchy:

  Model          SciQ     TruthfulQA
  Ministral-3B   9.6%     39.2%
  Llama-3.1-8B   4.8%     31.6%
  GPT-4o         2.0%      9.6%

Key observations:

(a) Model scale matters. GPT-4o is the most stable model across both datasets,
    with flip rates 4-5x lower than Ministral-3B. The 8B Llama model falls in
    between. This suggests that larger, more capable models develop more robust
    internal representations that are less susceptible to surface-level prompt
    variations.

(b) Task difficulty amplifies sensitivity. All three models show dramatically
    higher flip rates on TruthfulQA (which tests common misconceptions) than
    on SciQ (factual science questions). TruthfulQA flip rates are 3-8x higher
    than SciQ flip rates for the same model. When a model is uncertain about the
    answer content itself, even minor prompt changes can tip the decision.

(c) Accuracy and stability are correlated but not identical. GPT-4o achieves
    97.3% accuracy on SciQ with only 2.0% flips, while Ministral-3B achieves
    93.3% accuracy but 9.6% flips — a much larger gap in stability than in
    accuracy. This means accuracy alone is an incomplete picture of reliability.


2. Uncertainty-Flip Correlation
-------------------------------

We examine whether Logit Margin Variance (LMVar) — the variance of the gap
between top-1 and top-2 logits across prompt variants — predicts prediction
flips.

  Model          Dataset      Flipped Samples    Stable Samples
                              Mean LMVar (n)     Mean LMVar (n)
  Ministral-3B   SciQ         5.75 (24)          5.14 (226)
  Ministral-3B   TruthfulQA   1.35 (98)          4.77 (152)
  Llama-3.1-8B   SciQ         1.46 (12)          4.35 (238)
  Llama-3.1-8B   TruthfulQA   1.77 (79)          2.81 (171)
  GPT-4o         SciQ         2.01 (5)           3.54 (245)
  GPT-4o         TruthfulQA   5.62 (24)          6.32 (226)

Key observations:

(a) Counter-intuitive pattern: In most cases, samples that actually flip have
    LOWER LMVar than stable samples. This seems paradoxical but has a clear
    explanation — samples that flip tend to have low confidence across ALL
    variants (small logit margins uniformly), resulting in low variance. Stable
    high-confidence samples, by contrast, may have large absolute margin
    fluctuations even though the prediction never changes.

(b) Hidden instability is prevalent. Many samples never flip but exhibit high
    LMVar (>5.0), indicating that the model's confidence fluctuates substantially
    across prompt variants even when the final answer stays the same:

      Ministral-3B:  SciQ 38.0%, TruthfulQA 12.8%
      Llama-3.1-8B:  SciQ 32.8%, TruthfulQA 10.0%
      GPT-4o:        SciQ 20.8%, TruthfulQA 41.6%

    Notably, GPT-4o on TruthfulQA has the highest hidden instability (41.6%)
    despite having a low flip rate (9.6%). This means GPT-4o "knows" the right
    answer but its confidence in that answer swings significantly with prompt
    wording — a finding with implications for applications that rely on
    calibrated confidence scores.

(c) The point-biserial correlation r(LMVar, Flip) is weak across all
    conditions (ranging from -0.244 to 0.041), confirming that logit margin
    variance and prediction flips capture different dimensions of prompt
    sensitivity. Both metrics are needed for a complete picture.


3. Prompt Type Comparison
-------------------------

Per-variant accuracy across all models:

  Variant    Description       Ministral-3B         Llama-3.1-8B         GPT-4o
                              SciQ   TruthQA       SciQ   TruthQA       SciQ   TruthQA
  V0         Baseline         94.8%  60.4%         93.2%  59.2%         97.6%  84.8%
  V1         Reworded         94.8%  52.8%         93.6%  60.4%         97.2%  84.0%
  V2         Question-first   93.6%  57.2%         92.8%  62.0%         96.8%  85.6%
  V3         Minimal          90.0%  55.2%         92.4%  59.6%         97.6%  86.0%

Key observations:

(a) V3 (minimal, no instruction) tends to reduce accuracy for smaller models.
    Ministral-3B drops from 94.8% to 90.0% on SciQ when the instruction
    sentence is removed. GPT-4o is unaffected (97.6% for both V0 and V3),
    suggesting that larger models are less dependent on explicit instructions.

(b) Mean logit margin decreases from V0 to V3 for all models, indicating that
    models are generally most confident with the standard instruction-first
    format:

      GPT-4o SciQ:       V0=18.75  V1=18.22  V2=18.32  V3=16.47
      Ministral-3B SciQ: V0=7.32   V1=6.34   V2=5.35   V3=4.25

    The drop is especially steep for Ministral-3B (7.32 -> 4.25, a 42% decline),
    while GPT-4o's decline is more moderate (18.75 -> 16.47, a 12% decline).

(c) No single variant is universally best or worst across all model-dataset
    combinations. On TruthfulQA, V2 (question-first) actually gives Llama-8B
    its highest accuracy (62.0%), and V3 (minimal) gives GPT-4o its highest
    (86.0%). This highlights that prompt format effects are model-dependent
    and cannot be universally optimized.


4. USS (Uncertainty Stability Score) Analysis
---------------------------------------------

USS = 1 - std(entropy) / (mean(entropy) + epsilon) across prompt variants.
Higher USS means more stable uncertainty estimates.

  Model          SciQ     TruthfulQA
  Ministral-3B   -0.171    0.442
  Llama-3.1-8B   -0.241    0.246
  GPT-4o          0.194   -0.194

USS values are generally low and often negative, indicating that entropy
fluctuations across prompt variants are large relative to the mean — i.e.,
uncertainty estimates are fundamentally unstable under prompt perturbation.
This is partly due to the floor effect (logprobs outside top-N are
approximated as -20.0), but even accounting for this, the instability is real.

The negative USS values for several model-dataset pairs mean that the standard
deviation of entropy EXCEEDS the mean entropy, a strong signal of instability.


===============================================================================
PART II — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
===============================================================================

This study tested how sensitive LLM uncertainty estimates are to semantically
equivalent prompt reformulations, using three models (Ministral-3B, Llama-8B,
GPT-4o), two datasets (SciQ, TruthfulQA), and four prompt variants.

KEY FINDINGS:

1. Predictions are not robust to prompt wording.
   Even semantically identical prompts cause prediction flips in 2-39% of
   samples, depending on model and task difficulty. Smaller models and harder
   tasks are more affected.

2. Confidence is even less robust than predictions.
   Hidden instability — where the answer stays the same but confidence
   fluctuates significantly — affects 10-42% of samples. GPT-4o on TruthfulQA
   shows 41.6% hidden instability despite only 9.6% flip rate, meaning its
   confidence scores are unreliable even when its answers are stable.

3. Prompt format disproportionately affects smaller models.
   Removing the instruction sentence (V3) causes a 4.8pp accuracy drop for
   Ministral-3B on SciQ and a 42% decline in logit margin, while GPT-4o is
   virtually unaffected. Larger models are more resilient to surface-level
   prompt variations.

4. No universal "best" prompt format exists.
   Different models achieve peak accuracy under different prompt variants,
   especially on harder tasks. This makes prompt format a confound in any
   evaluation that uses a single fixed prompt.

5. Flip rate and confidence variance capture different phenomena.
   The correlation between LMVar and prediction flips is weak (|r| < 0.25),
   meaning both metrics are independently informative. A complete assessment
   of prompt sensitivity requires examining both prediction stability and
   uncertainty stability.

IMPLICATIONS:

- For practitioners: Do not trust a single prompt's confidence score. If
  calibrated uncertainty matters for your application (e.g., medical triage,
  autonomous decisions), test under multiple prompt phrasings.

- For researchers: Benchmark results obtained with a single prompt template
  may not generalize. Reporting performance variance across prompt variants
  should become standard practice.

- For model developers: Larger models show better prompt stability, but even
  GPT-4o exhibits significant hidden instability. Improving uncertainty
  calibration under prompt perturbation remains an open challenge.
