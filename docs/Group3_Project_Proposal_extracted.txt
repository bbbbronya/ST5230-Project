Abstract
This study investigates a core question: how sensitive are LLMs’ uncertainty estimates to perturba-
tions in semantically equivalent prompts? We test mainstream models (Llama-3 8B/70B Instruct,
GPT-5) on the SciQ and TruthfulQA datasets, designing semantically equivalent prompt variants
for each sample. Key metrics (Uncertainty Stability Score, Prediction Flip Rate, etc.) are used to
quantify the stability of uncertainty estimates and prediction variations, as well as analyze entropy
and logit margin variances and their correlation with prediction flips. We aim to identify inter-
model sensitivity differences and the impacts of different prompt variants on uncertainty stability,
and uncover hidden confidence instability if predictions stay stable but uncertainty estimates fluc-
tuate sharply. The findings will underpin the assessment of LLM system robustness, inform more
robust uncertainty estimation methods and prompt engineering optimization, and guide risk-aware
decision-making to boost decision consistency in real-world applications.
1. Research Question/Background
Large language models (LLMs) are widely used in tasks such as text classification and content
evaluation. In many applications, models are expected not only to produce predictions but also to
provide reliable uncertainty estimates, which are important for high-stake tasks . However, LLMs
interact with users through natural language prompts, and prior work has shown that small prompt
modifications can influence model predictions and evaluation results. While existing studies mainly
focus on how prompt perturbations affect prediction accuracy, it remains unclear whether model
uncertainty estimates themselves are stable under prompt variations. Therefore, understanding
1
the stability of uncertainty estimates under prompt perturbations is an important step toward
evaluating the robustness of LLM-based systems. Research question: How sensitive are LLM
uncertainty estimates to semantically equivalent prompt perturbations?
2. Motivation and Related Work
Although many methods have been proposed to estimate uncertainty in large language models, these
estimates are typically evaluated under a fixed prompt setting. In practice, however, LLM systems
interact with users through natural language prompts, and small variations in prompt wording
or formatting can lead to different model behaviors. If uncertainty estimates vary significantly
across semantically equivalent prompts, systems that rely on uncertainty thresholds may produce
inconsistent decisions. This raises an important question: are uncertainty estimates themselves
robust to semantically equivalent prompt variations?
Recent work studies uncertainty estimation in LLMs using prompting based methods. Some
studies prompt models to output confidence scores and evaluate calibration using metrics such as
ECE and AUROC (Xiong et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024), while others propose self evaluation
frameworks such asP(T rue) (Kadavath et al., 2022) or selective generation methods (Ren et al.,
2023). Sampling based approaches estimate uncertainty through output diversity under stochastic
decoding (Lyu et al., 2025), and prompt agreement methods measure uncertainty across multiple
prompt variants (Portillo Wightman et al., 2023). However, these studies mainly focus on improving
uncertainty estimation or calibration while the stability of uncertainty estimates under prompt
perturbations has received limited attention.

2. Prediction Flip Rate.We measure the proportion of examples for which the predicted
label changes across different prompt variants. This metric captures the sensitivity of model pre-
dictions to small prompt perturbations in wording or format.
3. Logit Margin V ariance.We compute the difference between the top-1 and top-2 logits
(logit margin) for each prompt variant and measure its variance across prompts. This metric reflects
the stability of model confidence under prompt variations.
4.2 Statistical protocol
We report the mean and standard deviation of uncertainty-related metrics across prompts and
analyze prediction flip rates and uncertainty variance across examples. These statistics allow us
to assess the stability of uncertainty estimates and identify cases where prompt variations lead to
significant changes in model confidence.
5. Analysis Plan
We will analyze how prompt variations influence model behavior in several ways. First, we measure
prediction stability by computing how often predicted labels change across semantically equivalent
prompts. Second, we examine uncertainty stability by analyzing variations in predictive entropy
and logit margins for the same input under different prompts. Third, we investigate whether higher
uncertainty variance correlates with prediction flips. Finally, we compare different prompt types
to assess their impact on uncertainty stability.
6. Expected Challenges, Limitations, and Ethics
This project may face several practical and methodological challenges:
1. API and model access constraints: API-based models may have rate limits or cost constraints,
which could limit the number of prompt evaluations. In addition, closed-source models may not
provide access to logits needed for uncertainty estimation.
2. Prompt design challenge: Designing semantically equivalent prompt variants is difficult, as
prompts must differ in wording or structure while preserving the same task meaning. Otherwise,
observed differences may reflect task changes rather than prompt sensitivity.
